## The Premise

Something shifted in how specialised knowledge gets valued. The information that once took years to accumulate (niche domain expertise, esoteric technical know-how) is now retrievable in seconds. AI-assisted search, knowledge graphs, and open-source tooling have made a lot of that hoarded expertise fungible.

I started thinking seriously about this when I joined **[Certie](https://certie.io/)**, which is working on standardising how skills and credentials are represented. The problem they're solving made me reflect on what "skilled" actually means when facts are cheap.

My tentative conclusion is that the scarce thing isn't information anymore. It's knowing what to do with it: assembling it, applying it across contexts, and making judgement calls when the answer isn't googlable.

## Why I Bet on Breadth

During undergrad at Nottingham Business School, a lot of my peers were drilling very narrow (a specific corner of finance, or one slice of CS) on the logic that depth equals security. That never quite made sense to me. If the thing you've specialised in can be replicated by an open-source library or an AI tool, the moat disappears fast.

So while I was studying **economics** (micro, macro, econometrics, quantitative methods) I was also deliberately picking up tools outside the syllabus: building things, joining a VC fund, leading teams, selling diamonds part-time. Not as a CV-padding exercise, but because I wanted to see if skills actually transferred the way I suspected they did.

They do, more than I expected.

## Where It Held Up

**Venture capital**, sourcing deals across the UK, UAE, and US, leaned heavily on being able to quickly get up to speed in a new industry, ask the right questions, and spot what mattered. No narrow specialisation helped much there; pattern recognition across domains did.

At **ChipHub** in Silicon Valley, I built a search tool for semiconductor procurement directors. I knew nothing about chips. What I did know was how to build a retrieval pipeline and figure out what a procurement director actually needed, which came from applying things I'd picked up elsewhere.

At **Certie**, leading a small team building an NLP pipeline (SpaCy NER, ESCO taxonomy mapping), the useful parts were less the ML specifics and more the ability to hold a product vision, work with a developer, and make tradeoffs under pressure.

The **diamond sales** role at Chisholm Hunter was the odd one out on paper. But it turned out to be where I learned the most about communication and relationship dynamics. Things that don't compress into a model weight.

## One Thing That Made It Work

The concurrent commitments only held together because the domains I picked had meaningful overlap in the underlying skills, even when the surface looked completely different. Economics, entrepreneurship, and venture capital share more than the obvious. That overlap meant I wasn't starting from zero each time I switched context.

## What I Actually Think

I don't think specialisation is dead. Deep expertise in the right things is still genuinely hard to replicate. But the *right things* have shifted toward orchestration, contextual judgement, and the ability to work across boundaries, and away from information hoarding.

I'm still figuring out what that means in practice. But so far, the bet on breadth has paid off more than I expected.
